Manifesto I

Two Honest Objects.

Dalisay is a Tagalog word. It means pure, distilled, refined — what remains after everything unnecessary has been gently set aside.

We make two things. Soap. Detergent.

The bar that touches your skin in the morning. The liquid that washes the linen that touches your skin at night.

Most of the world treats one as a luxury and the other as a chore. We refuse this distinction. Both are made by the same hands, from the same coconut, in the same atelier, with the same patience.

A clean shirt deserves as much grace as a clean face.

The Position

Brand Identity System

A house of cleansing. The bar for the body. The liquid for the home. Two products, one philosophy: that everyday acts of cleaning are also acts of dignity.

Category

The Filipino luxury cleansing house. Not a skincare brand. Not a wellness brand. Not a household brand either, in the conventional sense. A house that takes both ends of cleaning seriously — the body and the home — and treats them with the same craft, the same provenance, and the same paper-and-glass restraint.

The category we are building has a French precedent (Buly 1803, Compagnie de Provence) and a Japanese one (Daylight). It has no Filipino counterpart yet.

The Two Pillars

HUGAS

The bath. Cold-process soap bars, cured six weeks on cedar. Eight bars in the collection. For face, body, and hands.

LABA

The wash. Plant-derived liquid detergents and surface cleaners. Four formulations. For linen, dishes, floors, and the body's quietest companions — the clothes it lives inside.

Brand Pillars

Purity

No synthetic detergents in the bar. No phosphates, brighteners, or synthetic fragrance in the wash. Both lines pass the same ingredient standard.

Patience

The bar cures six weeks. The detergent ferments three. Time is the active ingredient in both.

Place

Every oil, every botanical, every hand is Filipino, named, and traceable to a province.

Promise

A bar that makes the bathroom smell like a small garden for six weeks. A detergent that makes a clean shirt smell, faintly, of lemongrass and sun. Two everyday rituals — washing the body, washing the clothes — restored to the standard they had a hundred years ago, before chemistry shouted at them.

The Pillar Distinction

Hugas (the soap line) uses warmer accents: cacao, sampaguita blush, muted gold. The bath feels intimate, cellular, close to skin.

Laba (the detergent line) uses cooler accents: linen blue, sage, and pearl. The wash feels architectural, breezy, fresh-air. The blue is borrowed from the colour of indigo-rinsed linen drying in the sun — the colour of the country's oldest laundry tradition.

Both lines share the gold seal, the charcoal type, and the ivory paper. A patron's shelf, when both lines are present, reads as a single quiet composition with two related but distinguishable tones.

The Refill Tower

Each Laba bottle is sold in three formats: the counter bottle (500ml, the daily-use vessel — apothecary glass with a brass pump or trigger), the refill (1L, in pearl card-and-aluminum laminate), and the tower refill (2L, for the Damit linen wash only, in a returnable aluminum jug).

The counter bottle is bought once. After that, only refills. The brand's mass-market efficiency comes from this: a small luxury counter bottle, used for years, refilled monthly.

What's Not In It

Across the entire Laba line: no phosphates, no optical brighteners, no synthetic dyes, no synthetic fragrance, no parabens, no SLS/SLES, no quats, no chlorine, no microplastics. Every formulation is biodegradable to OECD 301B standard. Septic-safe. Greywater-safe. Safe to use in homes with children, infants, and animals.

The full ingredient list — INCI names with Filipino translation — is printed on every bottle in 6pt Jost. The list is short enough to fit.

The Two Processes

How The House Makes Both

The bar takes nine weeks. The detergent takes five. Most of that time, nothing happens at all. That is the point.

— Hugas · The Bar Process —

i.
The Pressing
Pagpiga
Virgin coconut oil cold-pressed within hours of harvest. Cacao butter slow-pressed in Davao. No heat above body temperature. No solvents.
ii.
The Saponification
Paghahalo
Oils and lye meet at the precise temperature where they want to become one thing. The mixture is stirred by hand for forty minutes.
iii.
The Pour & Cut
Pagbubuhos at Paghihiwa
Poured into linen-lined wooden moulds. After 24 hours, the loaf is cut by hand with a single steel wire into 36 bars.
iv.
The Cure
Paghihintay
Six weeks on cedar shelves. The bar loses water, hardens, mellows. It becomes itself. This step cannot be shortened, and we have never tried.
v.
The Stamp & Wrap
Pagtatatak at Pagbabalot
Each bar pressed by hand with a brass die. Batch number stamped on the underside. Wrapped in unbleached paper, sealed with a wax band.

— Laba · The Detergent Process —

i.
The Glucoside
Pagbabago
Virgin coconut oil converted to coco-glucoside — a plant-based surfactant gentle enough for hands. The whole batch made in one stainless tank in our Pampanga annex.
ii.
The Fermentation
Pagsasama
Three weeks at room temperature. Bicol pineapple enzymes are cultured in cane sugar alcohol. They mature, multiply, and become the stain-lifting active. The detergent's equivalent of the cure.
iii.
The Blend
Paghahalo
Surfactant, enzyme, essential oil, and water are blended cold in small 200-litre batches. No heat. No preservative beyond the cane alcohol itself. Each batch is tested for pH, viscosity, and biodegradability before bottling.
iv.
The Settle
Pahinga
One week in glass carboys. The liquid clarifies. Bubbles dissipate. The blend becomes still. Nothing in cleaning has to be rushed.
v.
The Bottling
Pagbobote
Hand-filled into apothecary glass. Brass pumps or triggers screwed by hand. Label glued by hand. Wax band sealed by hand. Each bottle stamped with the batch number and the maker's initials.

"The same hands cure the bars and ferment the liquids. The house has one tongue and two voices."

— House Principle No. 2

The Long Practice

Sustainability & Stewardship

The category is the sustainability strategy. Soap and biodegradable detergent are, by their nature, among the most stewardable cleansing categories on earth — provided the packaging does not betray them.

Zero Plastic Across Both Lines

No plastic touches a Hugas bar or a Laba bottle. Counter bottles are glass; refills are pearl-card-and-aluminum laminate or returnable aluminum.

The Refill Atelier

In the Makati boutique: a marble counter where staff in linen aprons refill your Laba bottles from large carboys. 30% off every refill. The counter bottle is yours for life.

The Sliver Programme

Every boutique accepts the last sliver of any Hugas bar back. Slivers are melted into household bars and donated to partner schools.

OECD 301B Biodegradable

Every Laba formulation is independently tested and certified to OECD 301B "Readily Biodegradable" within 28 days. Septic-safe. Greywater-safe. Reef-safe.

The Garden Fund

One percent of every transaction funds botanical reforestation in our partner provinces — sampaguita in Laguna, calamansi in Mindoro, lemongrass in Negros, pineapple in Bicol.

The Provenance Card

A linen card in every gift set names the farms, the harvest months, the soap-maker, the fermentation lead, and the bottler. Sustainability told as a story, not a sticker.

The Working House

The Atelier & Boutique

A small townhouse in Makati. The boutique downstairs. The working atelier behind it. The brand is its workshop.

The Boutique

A renovated 1930s mestizo-style townhouse in Poblacion, Makati. The ground floor is the boutique — lime-washed walls, Vigan terracotta floor. Two facing shelves: the cedar wall for Hugas, the marble wall for Laba. A central marble counter for refills and for wrapping gifts. That is the entire store.

The Atelier

Through an open doorway behind the counter: the working atelier. Two soap-makers in linen aprons on the right side of the room — the wooden moulds, the cedar curing shelves, the wire cutter, the brass stamp. Two detergent-makers on the left side — the stainless tanks, the fermentation carboys, the bottling counter, the brass-pump assembly. Patrons can watch both lines being made, in the same room, on the same day. The making is the marketing.

The Saturday Class

Twelve seats per month. Three hours. Two tracks alternate: The Bar (cold-process soap-making) and The Wash (formulating a personal-recipe Damit linen wash). You leave with a bar or a bottle of your own making, embossed with the date. Booked three months in advance.

Future Houses

Year 1–2: Makati atelier-boutique.
Year 3–4: Cebu satellite (boutique only). Singapore (Tanglin). Tokyo (Aoyama).
Year 5+: Hong Kong, Bangkok, Paris (Marais), New York (NoHo).

Every overseas boutique is shelves-and-refill only. The atelier remains, always, in Pampanga. The bars and bottles travel; the making does not.

The Tongue

Brand Voice & Tone

Dalisay speaks the way a calm friend who reads poetry might speak about a bar of soap and a bottle of detergent. Specific. Brief. Warm without flattery. A little Filipino. Never marketing.

We Sound Like

  • Specific ("petals harvested before dawn in San Pedro")
  • Sensory ("a clean shirt smells, faintly, of the bath")
  • Confident without volume
  • Quietly Filipino, never costumed Filipino
  • Editorial — short sentences, then long ones
  • Honest about what a bar and a bottle do
  • Affectionate with the patron, never possessive

We Never Sound Like

  • "Game-changing," "must-have," "obsessed"
  • Hype, urgency, scarcity language
  • "Powerful enzymatic action," "next-generation cleaning"
  • "Kills 99.9% of germs" — even when it does
  • Faux-spiritual ("connect with your goddess")
  • Folkloric kitsch ("ancient Filipino secret of cleaning")
  • Defensive, apologetic, or self-deprecating

"Write as if the reader can already hold the bar and smell the linen. Your job is not to convince. Your job is to be remembered."

— Editorial Standard No. 4

The Image

Editorial Photography

Natural light, real skin, real linen, real bars, real bottles. Photography that looks accidentally found, not commercially staged.

The Bar Still

One bar, one shadow, one piece of unbleached linen. Side-lit by a window. Sometimes half-used, the patina visible. The bar should look like a small sculpture someone forgot to put away.

The Bottle Still

The Laba bottle on a marble counter beside a folded linen napkin and a clay water carafe. Late-afternoon sidelight. Never glossy. Never floating. The bottle photographed as if it belongs to a kitchen that has been in someone's family for thirty years.

The Laundry Picture

The brand's secret weapon. White linen drying on a clothesline strung between two coconut trees, with afternoon light coming through it. A woman's hand reaching to take a sheet down. A wicker basket below. The image that sells the Damit linen wash without ever showing the bottle.

The Hand & The Face

Filipino hands and Filipino faces — all of them. Tagalog, Bisaya, Ilocano, Tboli, Ifugao. Mestizo and morena. Hands holding bars. Hands wringing linen. Faces washing in cold water. The Filipino face has not been allowed to be the global luxury face. We change that.

The Hands Behind

Community & Artisan Empowerment

A luxury bar of soap and a luxury bottle of detergent are only as luxurious as the lives behind them. The brand is built on direct partnerships, named partners, and shared upside.

Direct Farm Partnerships

Every ingredient sourced from a named partner cooperative. Three-year guaranteed-price contracts. Cooperative names appear on every wrap and every bottle.

The Soap-Makers & The Detergent-Makers

Twenty full-time atelier workers in Pampanga — twelve on the Hugas line, eight on the Laba line. Paid above industry wage, with profit-share. Initials stamped on every product.

The Apprenticeship

Each year, eight apprenticeships for young Filipinos — four in soap-making, two in detergent formulation, two in retail. Full salary, housing in Manila, no bond.

The Garden Fund

1% of every transaction funds reforestation in partner provinces. Audited annually. Published openly.

The Naming Principle

Where ingredients have Filipino names, we use them. Tinawon, not "heirloom rice." Sukang Iloko, not "coconut vinegar," except in translation. Language is dignity.

Equity for Founders

By Year 5, partner cooperatives and atelier teams collectively hold a minority equity stake in the brand. This is not philanthropy. It is structure.

Export Strategy

The bars travel easily. The detergent bottles, less so — so the Laba line follows a different path to the world.

Phase I · Origin

Years 1–2. Makati atelier-boutique. Hotel-amenity partnerships with selected Filipino resort houses for both pillars — bars in the suite bathrooms, Laba bottles in the housekeeping carts.

Phase II · The Region · Hugas First

Years 2–4. Hugas bars are light, paper-wrapped, and easy to export. They lead: Singapore (Tanglin), Tokyo (Aoyama), Bangkok. Stockists at Lane Crawford, Isetan, Tangs.

Phase II.5 · Laba Follows

Year 3+. Laba bottles travel as part of the brand's standalone boutiques only — never to third-party stockists, where shipping costs and breakage would compromise the product. Where there is a Dalisay house, there is Laba.

Phase III · The Editors

Years 3–5. Liberty London, Le Bon Marché Paris, Bergdorf New York carry the Hugas line. Laba launches in those cities through Dalisay's own retail.

Phase IV · The Houses

Year 5+. Standalone Dalisay boutiques in Paris (Marais), New York (NoHo), Seoul (Hannam-dong), Los Angeles (Larchmont). Same cedar shelves. Same marble refill counter. Same eight bars. Same four bottles.

The Principle

We sell where we are wanted. We do not chase. We are not on Amazon. We are not on duty-free counters. We are on the cedar shelves and marble counters of selected boutiques in selected cities — patiently, permanently.

A Closing Story

Two Honest Objects

What we are really building, when we are building Dalisay.

I.

For most of luxury's history, soap and detergent have been considered different categories. Soap has been allowed the dignity of perfume; detergent has been pushed to the supermarket end-cap, where it has been screamed at by yellow labels and exclamation marks for the better part of a century. We have decided to refuse this distinction.

The same hand that washes the face washes the linen. The brand on both should match.

II.

Dalisay is a Filipino house of two pillars: Hugas, the bar for the body, and Laba, the wash for the home. They are made in the same atelier, by the same hands, from the same coconut, with the same patience. The bar cures six weeks on cedar. The detergent ferments three weeks in glass. Both refuse the chemistry that screamed at our grandmothers from the supermarket shelf — phosphates, brighteners, synthetic everything — and return to the chemistry that worked beautifully for three hundred years before that.

The radical proposition is the link between the two pillars: that everyday cleansing is a single ritual, not two, and that a country that treats the body with grace should also treat the home with grace, and the same brand should be able to serve both. This is the brand's structural insight, and it is what makes Dalisay a category of one.

III.

We are not building a soap company, and we are not building a detergent company. We are building a small piece of evidence — placed on cedar shelves and marble counters around the world — that Filipino craft, Filipino ingredient, and Filipino patience belong in the highest category of global luxury. That a country of seven thousand islands has been quietly perfecting, for centuries, two objects that the world has stopped taking seriously.

We are building a house that our grandchildren will inherit. A brand that pays the women in Laguna who pick sampaguita at four in the morning more than a brand has ever paid them, and the men in Camarines Sur who ferment pineapple enzyme more than a chemical company has ever paid them. A boutique in Makati where a Filipina teenager can walk in, look at the cedar wall of bars and the marble wall of bottles, and recognise that both of these objects — the one for her body, the one for her family's home — were made by people who look like her grandmother.

IV.

The word dalisay means pure. Distilled. Refined. What remains after everything unnecessary has been gently set aside.

This is what we are setting aside: the line between the body and the home. The pretence that a luxury bar of soap and a supermarket bottle of detergent must come from different worlds. The hype, the urgency, the cheap luxury, the borrowed glamor, the unpaid farmer, the un-named hand, the screaming label.

What remains: eight bars, four bottles, one atelier in Pampanga, one boutique in Makati, one country's worth of ingredient partners, and a brand built quietly enough to last a hundred years.

For the body. For the home. By the same hands.

That is Dalisay.
