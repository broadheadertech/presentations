i. narrative
— 01

The cinematic narrative.

A nation's first morning

SIBOL is not a soap. It is the first thing a country touches in the morning — and the last thing it trusts before sleep.

In every home — from the high-rise in BGC to the bamboo house in Bohol — there are two daily rituals that quietly equalize us.

A bar of soap on the skin. A scoop of detergent in the basin. Two small acts of dignity.

SIBOL begins in the soil. A coconut grower in Quezon. A calamansi farmer in Mindoro. A weaver of sampaguita garlands in Dangwa. A rice mother in Nueva Ecija. A beekeeper in Bukidnon. A lemongrass cooperative in Negros.

These are not suppliers. They are the first hands of the brand — co-authors of every bar. When SIBOL grows, they grow. Their daughters stay in school. Their sons stop migrating. Their barangay builds a roof that does not leak.

SIBOL is the Filipino household essential that the country has been waiting for: pang-masa but premium, modern but warm, profitable but principled. A soap that smells like home, looks like a gift, and carries the weight of a movement.

It is the simplest, most repeated acts in a Filipino day — washing one's hands, one's child, one's face, one's clothes — re-engineered into a daily vote for a fairer, cleaner, more hopeful Philippines.

This is not a product launch. This is the moment a country puts its hands on its own future.

The logo system.

A wordmark and a sprout

A custom lowercase wordmark, soft as a folded leaf — anchored by a single, characterful glyph: the SIBOL Sprout.

The lowercase decision is deliberate. Capital letters command. Lowercase invites. SIBOL must be felt, not declared. The "i" dot is replaced by a tiny rising shoot — the brand's secret signature, hidden at first, unforgettable once seen.

v. icon
— 05

The mascot: Si Sibol.

A character, not a cartoon

ix. retail
— 09

Shelf psychology.

Three meters · three seconds · three signals

A supermarket shopper makes a soap decision in roughly three seconds. SIBOL is engineered to win that exact window with three layered visual signals.

A monochrome green band ties the entire range. From three meters: one brand. From three centimeters: six choices.

Signal 01
The green band.

A single deep-forest strip across the top of every SKU creates an unbroken horizontal stripe when shelved together. At three meters, the eye sees one continuous flag of green — instantly mapped to "the natural one."

Signal 02
The italic ingredient.

Each variant's name is set in italic Fraunces — the only italic on the pack. The eye locks onto it. The ingredient becomes the brand's promise, repeated six ways.

Signal 03
The sprout dot.

The tiny sprout above the "i" repeats across every pack — a subliminal motif. Customers may never name it, but they recognize it the second time they walk past the aisle.

Signal 04
The matte versus the gloss.

Every competitor on the shelf is glossy plastic. SIBOL is matte uncoated paper. The hand reaches for what looks different — and feels honest.

xi. tingi
— 11

The sari-sari strategy.

The 50-gram and 70-gram beachhead

SIBOL wins the country at ₱12–₱15. Two gateway SKUs do the heavy lifting: the 50g soap sachet at ₱15 and the 70g detergent sachet at ₱12. Affordable enough for the tinderang nanay. Beautiful enough to sit beside Coca-Cola on the bilao.

The Sari-Sari Strip
A hanging strip of six. One peso of profit per piece for the store owner.

A vertical card strip — six 50g paper sachets, one per variant — hangs at the storefront. Designed to be ripped off one at a time. The sari-sari owner earns more per piece than on legacy soap. The customer gets a premium-looking purchase for ₱15.

Tindera Empowerment Program
The sari-sari owner is not a distributor. She is a co-owner of the movement.

Every sari-sari store that joins the SIBOL Tindera Program receives: a free storefront strip, a Si Sibol decal, a printed SIBOL shelf shelf-talker (₱15 / piece signage), and access to the SIBOL Tindera app — restocking, loyalty points, and a quarterly bonus tied to community sales targets. The local nanay becomes a brand ambassador with a real margin uplift.

Just the two daily rituals that define a clean Filipino home — done together, finally, by one brand.

xiv. impact
— 14

Social enterprise storytelling.

A movement, not a brand

SIBOL Communities is a parallel non-profit arm — six accredited Filipino cooperatives, one per variant, each receiving a guaranteed 5% of net SKU revenue plus a long-term supply contract.

Every variant has a face. Every face has a province. Every province has a community whose income, dignity, and tomorrow are tied to the bar you slipped into your shopping cart.

The QR code on every SIBOL pack opens a short film about the cooperative that grew it. The shopper does not buy a soap. The shopper enrolls — quietly, daily — in the rebuilding of provincial Filipino livelihood.

This is the engine that makes SIBOL structurally uncopyable. A competitor can match a fragrance. They cannot retrofit six cooperatives, three years of trust, and a hundred thousand pairs of community-rooted hands.
