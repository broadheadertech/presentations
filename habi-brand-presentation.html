Three Pillars
The Word
Habi. /ˈhɑː.bi/ Tagalog, n., v.

To weave; the act of weaving; that which is made by weaving.

In the Philippines, weaving is an ancestral craft — abaca in the south, inabel in the north, t'nalak among the T'boli. Habi names not only the cloth but the patience, the breath, the hand at the loom. We took the word because we make the same way — slowly, in small numbers, by hand. Two lines: one for the body, one for the cloth that wraps it.

— Packaging

A wrap, a cloth, a bar.

i.

The Outer Band

Matte uncoated stock, blind-embossed weave pattern. One column of editorial typography. Sealed with cacao wax.

ii.

The Cloth

Unbleached abaca or muslin, hand-folded around the bar, woven by named cooperatives in Ilocos and Abra.

iii.

The Bar

Cold-processed, cured four to six weeks, hand-cut. The HABI emblem stamped before cure. Edges are honest.

— Shelf Psychology

HABI wins by silence.

In a category of color and claim,
restraint becomes the loudest signal.
i.

Negative Space

Sixty to seventy percent of every face is empty. Surrounded by clutter, the empty band reads as confidence — the same way a fine fragrance does on a department-store counter.

ii.

Editorial Type

The serif wordmark, set wide and quiet, signals fashion-adjacent luxury — the visual register of fine fragrance, not mass beauty. Type is the price tag before the price tag.

iii.

Tactile Invitation

Matte uncoated paper, embossed weave, cloth peeking from the band. People pick HABI up. The pickup is the conversion event in artisan retail — and the most reliable one.

— A Closing Word —

A bar is a small thing.

It sits at the edge of the basin, or beside the laundry. It is used once, twice, a hundred times, then it is gone. Most people never think about it. HABI is for the people who do.

The bars are cold-processed in a small workshop in Manila — one line for the body, one for the cloth. The coconut oil comes from a family in Quezon. The honey is from beekeepers in Palawan. The calamansi from a grove in Batangas. The cloth that wraps each bar is woven by hand in a town in the north. Cut by hand. Cured for six weeks before it leaves us.

It costs more than a supermarket soap. It costs less than a department-store soap. It is worth, we think, what it costs — because of the time, and the source, and the hands.
